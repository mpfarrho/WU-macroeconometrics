# Syllabus: Advanced Macroeconometrics (Science Track)

Michael Pfarrhofer, *Vienna University of Economics and Business*. This syllabus applies to the summer term 2026. Go back to [README](https://github.com/mpfarrho/WU-macroeconometrics/blob/main/README.md) for navigation.

The macroeconomy is usually modeled in an impulse-propagation framework: *shocks* occur, *propagate* through the economy, and *affect observed variables*. The goal is to measure shocks, their dynamic (causal) effects, assess their relative importance, construct credible counterfactual scenarios, and predict future economic dynamics. The course materials provide a comprehensive toolkit for doing so econometrically and empirically.

## Contents
The course focuses on econometric methods used with macroeconomic data. The lecture consists of the following main building blocks:
- Univariate and (high-dimensional) multivariate time series analysis
- Bayesian econometrics, statistical computation and algorithms
- Structural (identification and estimation of dynamic causal effects) and predictive inference
- State-space models (if time permits)

Besides introducing students to such state-of-the-art techniques, an additional focus is to provide them with the necessary knowledge in statistical software (we will use `R`, [CRAN download](https://cran.r-project.org)) to conduct their own research projects. Students will be provided with theoretical inputs alongside empirical examples.

## Learning Outcomes
The course is aimed at students interested in working in academic or research positions, with the potential of publishing in refereed scientific journals. Students should gain in-depth knowledge about time series analysis, achieve a good foundational understanding of Bayesian econometrics, and be able to apply their knowledge independently for their own research papers, or thesis.

## Attendance Requirements and Assessment
Attendance is mandatory for this course, two absences will be tolerated. The course grade will be based on the following components:
- **Exam** (50 points)
- **Quizzes** (20 points)
- **Assignments** (30 points) in groups of max. 4 students.

To pass the course, a positive final exam score (50% or higher of total points) is required. The relevant material for the exam is defined by what has been taught in the course. The grading scheme is:
- **Excellent** (1): [89, 100] points
- **Good** (2): [78, 89)
- **Satisfactory** (3): [60, 78) points
- **Sufficient** (4): [50, 60) points
- **Fail** (5): [0, 50) points

## Teaching/Learning Methods
Course materials are self-contained and will be made available to participants in the form of slides and computer code. The slides are partly based on the following materials:
- Hamilton, J.D.: "[Time Series Analysis](https://press.princeton.edu/books/ebook/9780691218632/time-series-analysis-pdf)"
- Chan, J., Koop, G., Poirier, D.J. & Tobias, J.L.: "[Bayesian Econometric Methods](https://www.cambridge.org/highereducation/books/bayesian-econometric-methods/D48EA2B74340BE7899BB8FC50E72E22A?utm_campaign=shareaholic&utm_medium=copy_link&utm_source=bookmark)"
- Ramey (2016): "[Macroeconomic Shocks and Their Propagation](https://doi.org/10.1016/bs.hesmac.2016.03.003)" *Handbook of Macroeconomics*, volume **2**, 71-162
- Kilian, L. & Lütkepohl, H.: "[Structural Vector Autoregressive Analysis](https://doi.org/10.1017/9781108164818)" see also the [author website](https://sites.google.com/site/lkilian2019/textbook?authuser=0) for additional materials

A good understanding of classical econometrics and probability and statistics is highly recommended as a prerequisite. For example:
- Davidson, R. & MacKinnon J.G.: "[Econometric Theory and Methods](http://qed.econ.queensu.ca/ETM/)" chapters 1-3, 10
- Casella, G. & Berger, R.L.: "[Statistical Inference](https://www.routledge.com/Statistical-Inference/Casella-Berger/p/book/9781032593036)" chapters 1-6
